You can't fix what you can't measure.

  • Developer?

    You can't eyeball latency. It's too easy to get used to it. Your project will get more and more sluggish. Don't believe us? Just look at… most software, really.

  • Competitive gamer?

    Lower latency is an advantage. But how do you know if borderless windowed or fullscreen is faster? How do you squeeze the most from your setup?

  • Business?

    Users can feel when your software is snappy. It feels great! But your teams will sacrifice responsiveness if it's not tracked.

With Late Mate you can.

How Late Mate works

  1. Simulate the input

    Late Mate device takes a testing scenario and executes it, pretending to be a keyboard and a mouse. It's polled at 1kHz, the highest polling rate available for USB 2.0 devices.

  2. [Computer work goes here]

    From USB controller to software to the kernel to the compositor, your computer works hard to change pixels on the screen.

  3. Detect the screen change

    The device looks at a patch of your screen, waiting for a brightness change. It only takes Late Mate 0.5ms to know that the computer started reacting to the input.

  4. Time the difference

    Timing is done completely on-device, so the results are precise no matter what your computer's clock does.
